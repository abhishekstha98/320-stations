                             1. Introduction

                             In this global climate change scenario, the importance of accurate weather predictions is increasing. An
                             abnormal weather condition, such as cold weather, heavy snow, heavy rain, and drought, occur more frequently
                             worldwide. Weather change has a direct negative impact on daily quality of life, agriculture systems [1, 2], crop
                             management, energy utilization [3], image denoising [4], industries [5], the tourism industry [6], transportation
                             and logistics [7]. In the global scenario of agricultural landscape, weather-dependent decisions determine crop
                             yields and food production. Similarly, energy grids and utility systems are highly sensitive to weather
                             ﬂuctuations. Inaccurate forecasts can strain these systems, culminating in costly outages, infrastructure damage,
                             and disruptions in energy supply to homes and industries. Moreover, the transportation industry hinges on
                             timely and precise weather information for route planning, safety precautions, and minimizing disruptions due
                             to adverse weather conditions.
                                 There is a range of techniques and approaches for predicting atmospheric variables using various machine
                             learning and statistical methods. Existing weather forecasting models are based on physics and use
                             supercomputers to evolve the atmosphere in the future. To minimize this computational cost, several deep-
                             learning techniques have been proposed over the last few decades to forecast meteorological information. Some
                             recent methods are heatwave prediction [8], air temperature forecasting [9], high-resolution prediction of
                             spatiotemporal distribution of air pollution [10], precipitation prediction at a high resolution up to 12 hours
                             ahead [11], and rainfall prediction models [12–14]. GNN-based methods have demonstrated exceptional
                             performance in handling non-Euclidean weather data [15, 16]. Recently, GRAST [17] and HiSTGNN [18] have
                             introduced GNNs designed for multi-station weather forecasting, addressing the need for enhanced modeling of

interactions among meteorological variables from various stations. Besides forecasting the future, sometimes it
is essential to compute the missing atmospheric variable using other environmental factors. The speciﬁc variable
could be missed due to the absence or malfunction of the measuring device.
     Here we present a neural network to predict the current value of a speciﬁc atmospheric variable using other
environmental factors. Probabilistic forecasts predict the likelihood of weather conditions at a given time and
location. We proposed a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to classify the range of core atmospheric variables such
as rainfall, temperature, humidity, and air pressure using other correlated environmental variables. For the
atmospheric variables, a probabilistic outcome answers the question, “What is the range of probability of a given
variable occurring at a location and time based on other correlated environmental factors?”
     In developing countries, weather observations often include missing values, especially in diverse regions like
Nepal, where the plains, hills, and Himalayas create distinct climatic zones within short distances. These gaps,
caused by equipment malfunctions or human error, necessitate weather imputation, which estimates missing
data using techniques such as graph neural networks [19–21], regression trees, artiﬁcial neural networks [22],
rain gauge estimation [23], spatial-correlation approaches [24], statistical imputation with quality control
measures [25], and machine learning [26, 27]. Recent methods like ARIMA [20], LSTM [19], BiLSTM [28],
sequential imputation [29], dynamic time warping [30], and edge cutting [31] have further advanced this ﬁeld.
Weather imputation is crucial for maintaining continuous, reliable datasets and supporting decision-making in
weather-sensitive activities [45]. However, methods like Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) [32] and
interpolation [33] face challenges, including capturing complex weather phenomena, handling sparse data, and
managing the resource demands of these techniques [34].
     In this work, we introduce ImputeCast, a groundbreaking machine learning paradigm designed to tackle the
dual challenge of imputing partially missing attributes and predicting the range of entirely missing features.
Leveraging two distinct approaches, graph diffusion and graph neural networks (GNN), the method operates on
a systematically designed graph topology to enhance imputation accuracy. The graph diffusion technique
exploits information propagation across interconnected nodes, while the GNN approach harnesses neural
networks to capture intricate patterns in graph-structured data.
     The major contributions of this paper are as follows:

• We introduce a novel weather data imputation method that opens up new research avenues.
• Our approach leverages Graph Neural Network (GNN) technology to predict missing atmospheric variables
  by utilizing other environmental features. We outline a comprehensive GNN processing pipeline,
  encompassing data preparation, feature selection, and neural network optimization.
• This study presents a novel GNN architecture, speciﬁcally the GraphSAGE network, tailored for predicting
  missing weather data attributes, including rainfall, temperature, humidity, and atmospheric pressure, using
  available weather information. The GNN demonstrates proﬁciency in handling all four atmospheric variables
  while achieving a commendable level of accuracy.
• We introduced a feature selection method based on Pearson's correlation coefﬁcient that found comparatively
  better than the conventional approach.


2. Materials and methods

2.1. Dataset and preprocessing
Nepal's geographical landscapes exhibit a remarkable diversity in weather patterns and climate ﬂuctuations.
Spanning from the lowlands of Kechana Kalan, located at a modest elevation of 60 meters above sea level, to the
awe-inspiring heights of Mount Everest, the world's tallest peak towering at an astonishing 8,848 meters, the
country encompasses an astounding spectrum of altitudes. This geographical diversity encompasses an
extraordinary range of climatic nuances and meteorological phenomena. Within this work, we utilized historical
weather data sourced from 62 weather stations strategically distributed across diverse geographical regions
within Nepal. This dataset covers a time span of 39 years, encompassing daily weather records from January 1,
1981, through December 31, 2019. Remarkably, this dataset encompasses more than 883K data samples, making
it the most extensive and publicly available meteorological dataset [35, 36] within our knowledge. The dataset
comprised of several attributes, namely PRECTOT: Precipitation (mm/day) PS: Surface Pressure (kPa), QV2M:

     The dataset we used contains no missing values initially. However, we hypothesize that missing values are a
common occurrence in many datasets. To test the efﬁcacy of our model, we applied a random ﬁlter to
deliberately remove portions of the available data. This approach allows us to simulate missing data scenarios
and evaluate how well the model performs under these conditions.
     Following the dataset declaration, we proceed by representing the 62 weather stations as individual nodes
within a graph. These nodes are connected by edges, which are established based on similarities in altitude and
proximity to neighboring stations. We prepared the data to address a classiﬁcation problem involving four
atmospheric variables, each with its speciﬁc prediction range. Rainfall is classiﬁed into two categories, either
present or absent, while temperature is segmented into six distinct classes ranging from freezing to very hot.
Similarly, humidity is categorized into four classes—dry, moderate, humid, and very humid—while pressure is
stratiﬁed into four levels: very low, low, moderate, and high. In the dataset, the maximum temperature recorded
is 36°C. Accordingly, we categorized temperatures into the following ranges: below 0°C as freezing, 0–10°C as
cold, 10–18°C as cool, 18–25°C as moderate, 25–30°C as hot, and above 30°C as very hot. Similarly, humidity is
categorized into ranges of less than 40%, 40–60%, 60–80%, and above 80%. For pressure, the categories are less
than 70 kPa, 70–80 kPa, 80–90 kPa, and 90-100 kPa. While the categorization process may seem random, it is
purposeful and not arbitrary. Several studies underscore the importance of considering a broader spectrum of
weather variables. To understand the dynamics of vector-borne diseases [37, 38], diseases on plant and
vegetables [39], crop production prediction [40], minimizing extensive use of limited resources [41] utilities of
above categories is crucial.

2.2. Graph representation of weather data
A graph is a kind of non-Euclidean data structure which shows the relationship between entities; such entities
are known as the nodes and relationships are the edges of the graph. Formally, a graph can be deﬁned as
G = (V, E) where V = {v1, v2, K, vn} is the set of n vertices and E = {e1, e2, K, em}is the set of m edges. If the edges
on the graph are directed from one node to another, then the graph formed is the directed graph. Also, if the
strengths of the relationships between nodes are not the same, such graphs are weighted graphs. The
graph deﬁned above is an undirected and unweighted graph. In this weather imputation and missing variable
prediction task, we used weighted undirected graph, which is mathematically represented as G = (V, E, W)
where V is the set of vertices, E is the set of edges, and W is a matrix of size |V| × |V| representing the strength of
edges. In a graph signal processing task, a graph signal (graph feature) is a real mapping deﬁned on the
graph vertices V and edge weight (a real mapping on V × V ) is the connection strength of the vertices of the
graph. The matrix form of the edge weight is the weighted adjacency matrix W, D Î ∣ V ∣ ´ ∣ V ∣ , Dii = Sj wij is the

    In our approach, we conceptualize these weather stations as the nodes (vertices) of a graph, and the
connections between these nodes represent the edges. These edges facilitate the exchange of information
between the nodes, and weights are assigned to them to quantify the strength of the connection between any two
nodes. Importantly, our edge formulation takes into account both spatial and temporal aspects. By considering
both spatial relationships and temporal order in the edges, our approach provides a more comprehensive
framework for analyzing and understanding meteorological data, allowing us to capture both the physical
proximity of weather stations and the chronological sequence of events in our analysis.

2.2.2. Temporal edge construction
Temporal edges establish time-based relationships between nodes, connecting data from the same station at
consecutive points for enhanced temporal dependency and its weight tij is considered to be one.
    The weather data we have used is the temporal data, so to leverage the temporal information, spatial graphs
for every temporal point t = 1, 2, K, T are stacked and consecutive temporal points on the same station are
connected by an edge.
    The full lines in the graph indicate the spatial edges and the dotted lines indicate temporal edges. Therefore,
the graph obtained,called weather imputation graph (WIG), will have n × T nodes where n is the number of
weather stations and T is the length of temporal points. Furthermore, the size of the WIG is |E| = |ES| + |ET|,
where |ET| = n × (T − 1) is the number of temporal edges and |ES| is the number of spatial edges which is T ×
the number of edges on a spatial graph at a temporal point. Figure 1 gives a brief overview of the spatio-temporal
graph representing the weather stations and its temporal form.

3. Node feature selection method

In the context of graph-based weather data processing, the careful choice of node features holds the potential to
signiﬁcantly reduce computational costs and enhance model efﬁciency. Feature selection reduces
dimensionality, accelerates model training, and enhances interpretability. This process not only improves the
accuracy of predictions, beneﬁting weather forecasting and spatial data analysis, but also ﬁlters out noise and
ensures that computational resources are used efﬁciently.
    In the study [44], Genetic algorithm (GA) emerged as a more efﬁcient method than Wrapper approaches for
rainfall prediction. Malathi et al,malathi2020feature conducted a comprehensive analysis of various weather
feature selection strategies, including Information Gain (IG), Chi-Square (CS), Symmetric Uncertainty (SU),
Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), and GA, concluding that the selection of
these methods should be based on factors such as simplicity, stability, feature reduction, accuracy, and
computational efﬁciency. The correlation based feature selection [46] was used for precipitation type prediction.
However, it's essential to note that many of these methods require substantial domain knowledge and, in some
instances, signiﬁcant computational resources. In response, we have developed a straightforward yet highly
effective feature selection strategy using a statistical approach—Pearson's correlation coefﬁcient. This metric
allowed us to quantify the degree of linear association between each feature and the speciﬁc meteorological
parameter we sought to predict. The detail description can be found in algorithm 1.

4. Network architecture

In the work for missing atmospheric variable prediction, we employed several variants of Graph Neural
Networks (GNN). These encompass Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN), renowned for their ability to
capture local graph structure; GraphSAGE (Graph Sample and Aggregated Embeddings) [47], which adeptly
samples and aggregates information from neighboring nodes; Graph Attention Networks (GAT), leveraging
attention mechanisms to weigh node importance; and a hybrid approach known as SAGE-GAT, combining
both sampling and attention-based aggregation techniques. These distinct networks exhibit the ability to
capture both local and global dependencies within the atmospheric data graph, contributing to precise
predictions of missing atmospheric variables. In the context of predicting missing weather data variables,
GraphSAGE consistently outperforms other classiﬁers in all scenarios. Consequently, our subsequent discussion
predominantly focuses on the SAGE neural network for classifying the range of missing atmospheric variables.

5. Experiments and results

In order to ensure the comprehensibility and comparability of our research ﬁndings across different research
groups, we consistently present our experimental data in both visual and statistical formats. In this study, we
employed GCNs for imputing missing weather data and identifying absent atmospheric variables within a
speciﬁc range. This was accomplished by leveraging available meteorological data. We utilized several GNN
classiﬁers to predict missing variables, including temperature, humidity, air pressure, and rainfall. In the
experiment, we employed Pearson's correlation coefﬁcients for feature selection, optimizing the identiﬁcation
of these four crucial meteorological parameters. Our investigation involved testing our proposed system across
various missing data rates, even up to 90%, and the results demonstrated a high classiﬁcation score. To
quantitatively and qualitatively validate our ﬁndings, we employed a range of metrics [48], including accuracy,
F1-score, confusion matrix, Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) score, and t-SNE (t-Distributed Stochastic
Neighbor Embedding) plots. The confusion matrix proved valuable for reporting results in multi-class

6. Conclusion

As mentioned in the introduction, existing methods primarily focus on grid-based structures, which struggle
with sparse weather station data, and one of the widely used NWP methods is heavily inﬂuenced by initial data,
making it unsuitable for simultaneous spatial and temporal data gaps. Additionally, these methods do not
account for dependent weather variables when imputing missing data. Our method, which integrates
graph diffusion to handle randomly missing independent variables and utilizes Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
to forecast the ranges of key weather attributes like temperature, rainfall, humidity, and pressure, presents a
noteworthy advancement in meteorological modeling tailored for data-sparse environments. By introducing a

novel feature selection approach based on correlation coefﬁcients, we further reﬁne the precision and relevance
of our methodology in weather data analysis. Extensively trained on a comprehensive and diverse
meteorological dataset encompassing various altitudes and geographic locations, our model demonstrates
robustness. Signiﬁcantly, it achieves remarkable evaluation scores both in the presence and absence of weather
attributes, validating the effectiveness and versatility of our approach. Thorough validation through statistical
analyses and graphical assessments, employing a range of tailored metrics for classiﬁcation problems,
strengthens the credibility of our method and signiﬁes a substantial step forward in improving the accuracy and
reliability of weather attribute predictions in data-sparse regions.
